Wearable EEG

lightweight electronics for attention monitoring

Quick Note

I worked on this project in 2021 & 2022 as a part of the ECE Senior Design course at UT Austin. It was done with the support of the Lu Group, with whom I was also working at the time. Although I no longer work on this sort of thing (writing in 2024), I think this project gives a good idea of the sort of things that I spent my time on in undergrad.

The Project

Electroencephalography (EEG) and electrooculography (EOG) are interesting biological monitoring techniques. EEG measures electric voltage potentials at the surface of the skin caused by neuronal activity, and EOG measures the voltages due to ocular movement.

There’s a bunch of systems for measuring these signals, such as the BrainVision LiveAmp. Many of these existing products have bulky electronics, require a wired interface to a computer, or are uncomfortable.

In particular, we wanted to make a system that could monitor EEG signals (and EOG signals), transmit that data wirelessly, and fit nicely under a standard US Army flight helmet (the group’s research was partially funded by the Army Futures Command). We also wanted to use the data to classify the user’s mental state as either attentive/vigilant or not.

You can see a very poorly-drawn diagram of the system that I had made at the time:

A poorly-drawn system diagram showing what we wanted to build.

To do this, we designed a flexi-PCB with the ADS1299 to measure the EEG signals and an NRF52832 to transmit those signals over bluetooth. First though, we designed a test PCB so that we could verify that the hardware selection and code was going to work well. This early design just contained a 2.54mm header like you would find in a breadboard circuit, as well as an additional debugging interface.

Once we verified that the hardware was workable, we designed the flexible printed circuit and an e-tattoo to attach to the FPC, which contained thin graphene traces and electrodes.

Left: a render of an early design that was used to test the ADS1299 and serve as a platofrm for code development. Right: the flexible PCB for recording EEG measurements, with most of its components soldered on. It had contacts on the reverse side that could be atteched to the rest of the e-tattoo with conductive 2-sided tape.

Because we were tight on time, we sized the circuit to Sammy’s face and used him in any experiment that required the full assembly.

Of course, we wanted to see how our system held up to the commercial stuff. We set up both our system and a BrainVision LiveAmp system to simultaneously measure EEG signals and hooked them both up to the same set of electrodes on my face.

Left: A side view of Sammy wearing the e-tattoo, showing the battery and the wire that runs behind the ear for a reference electrode. Middle: the EEG system fits underneath an army flight helmet. Right: me wearing a bunch of electrodes so that we can compare our EEG device to with a BrainVision LiveAmp commercial EEG device by taking simultaneous measurements.

Unfortunately I don’t think that I have the raw data any more. I believe the reason why the EOG channels showed better correlation was probably due to the larger amplitude of those signals (on the order of mV) compared to the EEG signals (which are often multiple degrees of magnitude smaller). However, our system was able to identify key features like the alpha/beta/gamma waves.

Using these signals (and a bunch of filtering, of course), we tried a few different methods of predicting the wearer’s alterness level. These included machine-learning based approaches and hand-tuned heuristics. To gether data for all this, we made Sammy take the Mackworth Clock Test for somewhere around 45 minutes straight, then sent him to lay down and doze off while wearing the EEG system.

Sammy wearing the EEG e-tattoo on the ECE Department's senior design demo day.

After all that, I don’t think we were able to find something better than PERCLOS, which is what we ended up demoing for the senior design presentation. I was somewhat disappointed by that, but after completing undergrad, I largely stopped working on these sorts of embedded systems projects, so I didn’t spend much more time exploring alternatives beyond that point.

I may publish the code / design files for this project at some point soon.
